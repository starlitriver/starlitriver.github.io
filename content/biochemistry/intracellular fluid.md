Intracellular fluid is distinguished from [[extracellular fluid]], which is found between cells in multicellular organisms and serves to conduct nutrients, waste products, and other substances throughout the [[tissues]] of the body. Relative to extracellular fluid, intracellular fluid is slightly more [[acid|acidic]]: the pH of intracellular fluid is between roughly 6.8 and 7.4, depending on the [tissue](https://www.britannica.com/science/tissue) and [cell](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-biology) type, whereas extracellular fluid pH is between about 7.3 and 7.5. Intracellular and extracellular fluid are separated by the [cell membrane](https://www.britannica.com/science/cell-membrane), which is composed of a semipermeable [lipid](https://www.britannica.com/science/lipid) bilayer.
