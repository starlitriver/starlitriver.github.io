Vascular tissue is a complex conducting tissue, formed of more than one cell type, found in vascular plants. The primary components of vascular tissue are the xylem and phloem. These two tissues transport fluid and nutrients internally.


**Transpiration:**

- The movement of water up the xylem and its exit via the stomata as the passive process of transpiration.
    

**Translocation:**

- The movement of nutrients created in the leaves to other areas of the plant. This tends to take place in the phloem from a source to a sink. Translocation of glucose in the phloem can be broken up into four different steps:
    

1. Glucose is produced in the leaf (the source) and pumped into the companion cells where they diffuse into sieve cells of the phloem.
    
2. Increased concentration in the sieve cells causes water to diffuse in from the xylem increasing turgor pressure in the sieve cells of the phloem.
    
3. The increase in turgor pressure pushes the liquid in the phloem throughout the plant, where glucose will be actively transported into the required cells (the sinks). The sink cells are the fruit and root cells.
    
4. Once glucose is unloaded into the sink cells, the concentration in the phloem is reduced and water will diffuse back into the xylem.
    

**Roots function:**

- The root system has two major functions - to provide stability and support to a plant and to absorb the water and minerals from the soil that are required for complex plant life.
    
- The root system has many different branches which increase the overall surface for absorption.
    
- Root hair cells have finger-like projections that extend outwards, further increasing the surface of roots.
    

**Adhesion:**

- Water is attracted to and moves across many surfaces. This is why water rises up paper, rises up capillary tubing, and forms a meniscus in a test tube.
    
- As water is lost from the stomata its loss creates a negative pressure at the top of the xylem vessel. This draws water up the xylem vessels, in a similar way to drink moving up a straw when a low pressure is created by sucking it.
    

**Regulation of water loss:**

- The guard cells of a stomata function very similar to the tube in a bicycle tyre. When the tyre (or guard cell) contains fluids under high pressure, it becomes turgid and central space opens up. When the tyre loses fluid, it becomes flaccid and the stomata closes.
