calculus has two major branches, [differential calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus "Differential calculus") and [integral calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_calculus "Integral calculus")

the former concerns instantaneous [rates of change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_of_change_(mathematics) "Rate of change (mathematics)"), and the [slopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), while the latter concerns accumulation of quantities, and [areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") under or between curves. These two branches are related to each other by the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"), and they make use of the fundamental notions of [convergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergence_(mathematics) "Convergence (mathematics)") of [infinite sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_sequence "Infinite sequence") and [infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)") to a well-defined [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)")
