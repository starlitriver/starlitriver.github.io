the rational root theorem, also known as the rational root theorem, is quite confusing
but for some reason, it works

a polynomial $f(x)$, the possible solutions (roots) of the function lie in $x=\pm\dfrac{\text{set of factors of c}}{\text{set of factors of leading coefficient}}$
this way, we make a set of possible solutions for x, and we substitute each of them as $x$ to try and factor something out

remember to try the values that are closer to zero first


worked example: factor $x^3-2x^2-5x+6$
possible solutions for x lie in $x=\pm\dfrac{\text{set of factors of c}}{\text{set of factors of leading coefficient}}$
$x\in\{1,-1,2,-2,3,-3,6,-6\}$
try long division by $(x-1)$
$(x-1)(x^2-x-6)$
$(x-1)(x+2)(x-3)$


worked example: factor $2x^3+x^2-5x+2$
possible solutions for x lie in $x=\pm\dfrac{\text{set of factors of c}}{\text{set of factors of leading coefficient}}$
$x\in\{1,-1,2,-2\}$
try long division by $(x-1)$


worked example: factor $x^3+3x^2-6x-8$
possible solutions for x lie in $x=\pm\dfrac{\text{set of factors of c}}{\text{set of factors of leading coefficient}}$
$x\in\{1,-1,2,-2,4,-4,8,-8\}$
try long division by $(x-1)$
we get a remainder
try long division by $(x+1)$
$(x+1)(x^2+2x-8)$
$(x+1)(x-2)(x+3)$


worked example: factor $x^3+2x^2-9x-18$
possible solutions for x lie in $x=\pm\dfrac{\text{set of factors of c}}{\text{set of factors of leading coefficient}}$
$x\in\{1,-1,2,-2,3,-3,6,-6\dots\}$
try long division by $(x-1)$
we get a remainder
try long division by $(x+1)$
we get a remainder
try long division by $(x-2)$
we get a remainder
try long division by $(x+2)$
$(x+2)(x^2-9)$
$(x+2)(x+3)(x-3)$
